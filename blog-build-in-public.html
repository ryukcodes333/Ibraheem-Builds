Why I Build in Public

Sharing half-finished work is uncomfortable. You're never sure it's worth posting. But the feedback loop from building publicly is something you can't replicate any other way.

The Resistance

Every time I'm about to post something I've built, I have the same thought: "This isn't good enough yet." The UI isn't polished. The code is messy. Someone's going to point out a bug in public. There's always a reason to wait.

But waiting is expensive. Every day you don't ship, you lose the feedback that would have made the thing better. And "better" is a moving target anyway — you'll always find something to fix if you're looking for it.

What Actually Happens When You Post

I posted my first bot when it could only do three commands. I was embarrassed. I expected nothing, or worse, criticism. What I got instead:

  • Three people asked if they could test it
  • Someone pointed out a bug I hadn't seen
  • One person suggested a feature I actually added

None of that would have happened if I'd waited until it was "ready." There is no ready. There's only shipped and not shipped.

It Keeps You Accountable

When you post about what you're building, you create a public record. You said you were going to build this. That's enough pressure to actually finish it. I've abandoned private projects dozens of times. I've never abandoned something I posted about publicly.

The audience doesn't even need to be large. Ten people watching is enough. You're not building a following — you're giving yourself a commitment device.

You Learn Faster

Building in public forces you to explain what you're doing. Explaining forces you to understand it properly. I've caught misunderstandings in my own thinking by trying to write a clear description of what I built.

It also puts you in contact with people who've already solved the problems you're working on. The developer who replied to my post about session management had exactly the fix I needed. That's not luck — that's what happens when you're visible.

The best way to get a good answer on the internet is not to ask a question — it's to post the wrong answer and let someone correct you.

What I Post

I try to post things that are real. Not polished demos — actual screenshots of things working (or breaking). A clip of the bot responding. A before/after of a UI improvement. The concrete thing, not the idea of the thing.

Nobody is interested in "I'm building an app." Everybody is interested in "here's the weird bug I just fixed." Specificity is the point.

If you're sitting on something you've built and haven't posted it yet — post it. The embarrassment lasts about 10 minutes. The feedback lasts forever.
